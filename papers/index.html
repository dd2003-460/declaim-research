The Attention Proxy Gap: A Systematic Null Study of Attention-Based Trading Strategies

Behavioral Asset Pricing Null Results Market Efficiency

The hypothesis that limits on investor attention generate exploitable price delay has become a cornerstone of behavioral asset pricing. We test this proposition across six independent signal pathways—Form 4 insider trading clusters, Google Trends retail search intensity, arXiv preprint bursts, SEC 13F institutional ownership clusters, regime-aware factor rotation, and cross-asset lead-lag relationships. Across all six pathways we find uniformly null results. The null is structured, not noisy: one pathway returns exactly zero effect (p = 1.0), two are catastrophically underpowered, one is invalidated by autocorrelation-induced false positives, and the remaining two suffer structural identification failures. We formalize these findings as the Attention Proxy Gap and propose an information-theoretic framing—computational sufficiency—under which mispricing cannot persist for publicly observable, algorithmically tractable information in liquid markets.

Regime-Conditional Factor Rotation: When Known Factor Premia Fail to Generate Outperformance

Factor Timing Regime Switching Quantitative Investing

We examine whether regime-conditional factor premia can be exploited through a dynamic rotation strategy, using a four-state regime classification (crisis, bull_volatile, bear_trending, bull_trending) applied to six US equity factor ETFs over the 2020–2026 period. We document strong regime-dependent factor performance: in crisis regimes (VIX ≥ 35), USMV (minimum variance) delivers significant positive abnormal returns (Cohen's d = 0.325, p = 0.0109), while in bull trending regimes, QQQ achieves an annualized Sharpe ratio of 1.512 (p = 0.0064). However, a regime-conditional factor rotation strategy underperforms a simple equal-weight benchmark by −6.95% cumulative abnormal return after 5 basis point transaction costs (p = 0.8156). We argue that the failure is not due to absent signals but to the selective failure of regime classification at transition points, when the marginal value of correct allocation is greatest.
